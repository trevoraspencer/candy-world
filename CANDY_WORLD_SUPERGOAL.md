# Candy World End-to-End Rebuild Goal Brief

## Mission

Transform **Sweet, Sweet World** into a substantially more polished, interactive, and cohesive single-player voxel sandbox that follows the interaction language and progression rhythm that make Minecraft compelling while preserving the project's original Candy World identity and the warm, playful spirit of a game made by a parent and child. The finished game should still be recognizable as this project—not a replacement project, an unrelated engine demo, or a copy of Minecraft's copyrighted art, audio, text, or code.

This is a brownfield improvement program. Preserve working systems, evolve them incrementally, and keep the game playable after every phase. Do not rewrite the project in a framework merely to make it feel more “professional.” Vanilla HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Canvas APIs, Web Audio, Web Workers, IndexedDB, and WebGL2 are sufficient for the intended result.

## Current Baseline

The repository is already a serious foundation: a dependency-free WebGL2 voxel renderer; a fixed 256×64×256 world divided into 16×16 chunks; procedural terrain and five biomes; ambient occlusion, frustum culling, fog, water, clouds, particles, and day/night color changes; mining and block placement; a 36-slot inventory and hotbar; basic, crafting-table, and furnace recipes; survival meters; quests; creative mode; nine mob types; villagers and trading; pet taming and naming; waystones; and versioned `localStorage` persistence with backup and sanitization. The existing browser build runs around 60 FPS in the reviewed scene with no console warnings or errors, and every JavaScript file passes `node --check`.

The primary problems are not lack of code or a weak foundation. They are flat rendering, low material contrast, little first-person embodiment, no audio, shallow survival progression, a solid underground with no caves, unused combat equipment, world systems that rarely affect one another, and technical constraints that prevent larger worlds and reliable long-term saves.

## Non-Negotiable Constraints

- Keep the game single-player and fully playable offline.
- Keep the existing no-build, no-runtime-dependency delivery model unless a future dependency is proven essential and explicitly justified. Prefer browser-native APIs and Node's built-in test runner.
- Preserve the Candy World theme. Use original candy analogues, names, textures, models, and sounds; do not copy Minecraft assets.
- Preserve existing features unless a replacement demonstrably improves the same player need. Pets, quests, trading, creative mode, and the current world should not casually disappear.
- Preserve old saves through explicit schema migrations. Never silently discard an existing world or inventory.
- Keep Creative mode easy and joyful. Make Survival progression meaningful without taking Creative away.
- Provide Peaceful or Cozy options so a child can explore without unwanted punishment.
- Keep keyboard and mouse controls working throughout. All new menus must be keyboard accessible and must not strand pointer lock or held input state.
- Keep common gameplay near 60 FPS on the current target machine. Establish measurable frame-time budgets before increasing world or entity density.
- Every phase must leave the project syntactically valid, browser-loadable, save-compatible, and free of new console errors.

## Required Execution Approach

Implement the work in dependency order rather than attempting thirty disconnected patches. Start by adding a lightweight safety net with Node's built-in `node:test` for pure logic such as seeded randomness, inventory transactions, save migrations, recipe matching, and fixed-step simulation. Introduce explicit subsystem boundaries or namespaces opportunistically—world, simulation, renderer, entities, items, persistence, input, audio, and UI—but do not stop progress for a wholesale architecture rewrite. Use data registries and gameplay events such as `blockHit`, `blockBroken`, `itemDropped`, `itemPickedUp`, `entityDamaged`, `itemCrafted`, `nightStarted`, and `questAdvanced` so visuals, audio, quests, and saves can react without hardcoded cross-file calls.

Recommended execution order:

1. Safety net and baseline performance/visual captures.
2. Render-resolution, lighting, transparency, sky, and liquid foundations: items 1, 4, 5, and 6.
3. Texture and biome art direction: items 2 and 3.
4. First-person feel, feedback, audio, and HUD: items 7–10.
5. Title flow, onboarding, and world rules: items 11–13.
6. Harvesting and physical loot: items 14–15.
7. Underground, combat, and night gameplay: items 16–18.
8. Crafting, stations, and building utilities: items 19–21.
9. Farming, structures, mechanisms, progression, and living-world behavior: items 22–26.
10. Deterministic worlds and durable persistence: items 27–28.
11. Fixed simulation, collision hardening, chunk streaming, and workers: items 29–30.
12. Final polish, accessibility, performance, save-migration, and regression audit across all thirty items.

## The 30 Required Improvements

### 1. Render at device-pixel resolution with scalable quality

The current `resizeCanvas()` assigns the WebGL backing buffer directly from `window.innerWidth` and `window.innerHeight`, while the context is created with antialiasing disabled (`js/webgl.js:5-14`). On a DPR-2 display, the reviewed 1280×720 viewport therefore rendered only a 1280×720 backing buffer instead of using the available physical pixels, making every edge and texture softer than necessary. Update resizing to multiply CSS dimensions by a capped device-pixel ratio, keep CSS display size unchanged, recreate the projection from the true backing aspect, and expose a persistent render-scale option such as 50%, 75%, and 100% so lower-powered devices can trade sharpness for speed. Evaluate optional MSAA rather than enabling it blindly, and make screenshots part of verification. This item is complete when a DPR-2 1280×720 viewport can render at an effective 2560×1440 at 100% scale, resizing and fullscreen changes remain correct, the HUD retains its CSS size, selectable lower scales work, and the representative scene remains within the agreed frame-time budget without console or WebGL errors.

### 2. Rebuild the procedural texture atlas around readable materials

The atlas generator currently relies heavily on a flat fill plus sparse deterministic noise, and many unrelated materials occupy nearly the same pale-pink value range (`js/webgl.js:263-399`). This makes the world visually muddy even though it contains many block types. Retain the original 16×16 pixel-art discipline and offline canvas generation, but create material-specific pattern functions instead of one generic noise treatment. Graham-cracker dirt should have layered crumbs, strawberry-wafer grass should have a readable top edge, marshmallow stone should have soft pores and cracks, chocolate wood should show grain and rings, hard-candy glass should have a crisp border and shine, frosting should visibly drip, and ores should have unmistakable crystalline silhouettes. Generate two to four stable coordinate-selected variants for common terrain so large surfaces do not visibly repeat, and ensure inventory icons use the same art. This item is complete when common blocks can be identified in unlabeled daytime and nighttime screenshots from six to ten blocks away, variants remain deterministic across reloads, atlas sampling has no seams or bleeding, and no Minecraft texture or close derivative has been copied.

### 3. Give every biome a distinct candy ecology and silhouette

The current five biomes mostly vary terrain height, a surface/filler block, a tree form, and a palette (`js/world.js:57-123,164-273`). A player should be able to recognize location from the world itself rather than reading the debug label. Develop a coherent identity for each region—for example Candy Cane Woods, Gumdrop Meadows, Marshmallow Peaks, Truffle Deep Dark, and Sherbet Coast—and give every biome at least three exclusive small props, one exclusive resource or creature behavior, a distinctive terrain or skyline feature, a specific weather/ambient palette, and a recognizable structure family. Add lightweight non-solid vegetation through crossed quads or small cuboids: licorice grass, sugar flowers, jelly bushes, wafer shards, cookie crumbs, candy-shell boulders, frosting drifts, and giant dessert formations. Vary house and waystone silhouettes rather than recoloring the same geometry, and reserve clear paths through dense regions. This item is complete when blind screenshots reliably distinguish all five biomes, transitions feel intentional rather than abrupt, every exclusive prop is deterministic and collision-safe, and the added density does not materially harm traversal or frame time.

### 4. Add real sunlight, skylight, block light, and emissive materials

Current terrain and mob shading is a fixed lookup based on cube-face direction, then multiplied by one global daylight scalar (`js/webgl.js:102-159`, `js/effects.js:8-41`). It suggests depth but cannot create bright mornings, dark caves, glowing furnaces, or a meaningful reason to place lights. Add a time-dependent sun direction and separate sky-light and block-light values in chunk data or mesh vertices. Begin with bounded propagation suitable for the existing chunk model, mark select materials as emissive, and update affected local regions when a light is added or removed. Candy adaptations should include glowing lollipop lamps, sugar crystals, active ovens, and softly luminous Deep Dark growths. Use gamma-aware color handling so night becomes atmospheric without making the game unreadable. This item is complete when enclosed caves are genuinely dark, opening a cave to the sky changes its illumination, placing and removing a lamp updates nearby blocks across chunk boundaries, active furnaces visibly glow, dawn/noon/dusk/night have different directional light, and lighting updates are budgeted so rapid placement cannot freeze the browser.

### 5. Build a layered sky and biome-aware candy weather system

The current world clears to one interpolated color and renders a single moving cloud plane (`js/effects.js:8-41`, `js/webgl.js:594-650`). Replace this prototype presentation with a dedicated sky pass containing a horizon-to-zenith gradient, wafer sun, peppermint moon, stars, and multiple cotton-candy cloud layers with different speeds and depths. Add deterministic or scheduled weather states such as sprinkle rain, powdered-sugar snow, light sherbet mist, and occasional candy-storm ambience, selected by biome and time of day. Weather should affect fog color and sound but should not initially attempt expensive full shadowing or complex accumulation. Expose particle and flashing intensity settings and honor reduced motion. This item is complete when four times of day are recognizable without HUD text, sun and moon move consistently with the clock, clouds no longer resemble one ceiling sheet, at least three weather types start and stop cleanly, weather never occurs in nonsensical biomes, saves restore time/weather safely, and the heaviest weather remains within ten percent of the baseline frame time.

### 6. Animate liquids and correct transparent rendering

Water currently lowers each water vertex by a fixed amount, samples a static atlas tile, and uses fixed alpha; glass is marked transparent but is still emitted into the ordinary non-water mesh (`js/webgl.js:161-188`, `js/world.js:630-679`, `js/render.js:150-195`). Split rendering into opaque, alpha-cutout, and translucent passes. Keep depth testing, disable depth writes only where needed, and sort translucent chunks or faces back-to-front. Pass time into the liquid shader for restrained UV flow and top-surface ripples, distinguish side and top behavior, add underwater color/fog, and create candy liquids such as strawberry syrup without making them visually identical to water. Do not add full fluid simulation yet unless item 21's block-update foundation can support it safely. This item is complete when glass correctly reveals blocks and entities behind it, overlapping water surfaces do not visibly reorder while the camera turns, shore faces remain stable, liquid animation does not make the terrain appear to slide, entering water changes the visual atmosphere, and transparency continues to perform at the existing view distance.

### 7. Add first-person embodiment and responsive camera feel

The current view is mathematically rigid: a fixed 70-degree projection at the player eye and no rendered hand or selected item (`js/webgl.js:5-10`, `js/player.js:140-185`, `js/render.js:78-218`). Add a small first-person render layer after the world pass that shows a blocky Candy World hand and an appropriate representation of the selected tool, treat, or block. Drive subtle walking bob from horizontal velocity, add an eased sprint FOV, landing dip, idle breathing, selection sway, and distinct action clips for mining, placing, attacking, eating, and interacting. Avoid excessive shake, and decouple these presentation effects from physics so disabling them does not change gameplay. This item is complete when the held object always matches the hotbar, every core verb has a readable animation, sprint and landing transitions ease smoothly, animations never clip through menus or remain stuck after pointer-lock loss, view motion can be reduced or disabled, and mouse sensitivity/FOV settings persist without breaking targeting.

### 8. Move mining and interaction feedback into the world

Mining currently removes a completed block immediately and draws its main crack/progress feedback as a screen-centered two-dimensional overlay (`js/render.js:85-114,236-300`). Create four to eight crack stages rendered on the targeted block faces so the damage remains attached while the camera moves. Emit deterministic material-colored debris at the actual hit point, add a small placement poof or squash, flash and knock back damaged entities, animate crafted outputs, and show short local reactions for trade, tame, pickup, and quest completion. The particle renderer currently stores colors but draws a uniform pink line style, so preserve per-particle color and allow a small set of cheap render shapes (`js/effects.js:206-287`). This item is complete when feedback begins within 100 milliseconds of input, cracks stay attached to the correct block and reset on target change, every common material family emits matching debris, particle and camera effects respect configurable caps, and rapid mining or repeated failures cannot grow arrays or GPU uploads without bound.

### 9. Add a procedural, offline Web Audio soundscape

The game currently has no sound system, which removes a major source of weight, spatial awareness, reward, and charm. Initialize one `AudioContext` only after the first explicit play gesture and build short original sounds from oscillators, filtered noise, envelopes, and small generated buffers. Provide different footstep and break/place families for wafer, frosting, stone, glass, wood, sugar, and liquid; add tool swings, pickup chimes, eating, crafting, furnace, UI, quest, trade, tame, mob, weather, Warden proximity, and damage sounds; and use sparse biome ambience rather than continuous noisy loops. Centralize sound reactions on gameplay events instead of scattering direct calls. This item is complete when every core verb has an appropriate sound, at least four material families are audibly distinguishable, positional world sounds attenuate sensibly, master/music/effects sliders and mute persist, suspended contexts resume only after a user gesture, reduced audio environments fail silently, and no sound resembles copied Minecraft audio.

### 10. Redesign the HUD as a candy-voxel survival interface

The existing HUD is cheerful but resembles a rounded web application more than an in-world voxel game, uses platform-dependent emoji, displays health and hunger with nearly identical glyphs, places several controls near one another at the top center, and exposes debug coordinates/FPS continuously (`index.html:21-35`, `css/style.css:919-1039`, `js/render.js:224-233`). Build original generated pixel icons, use dark chocolate outlines and restrained frosting highlights, place health and hunger immediately above the hotbar, briefly show the selected item name, and move pets, quests, difficulty, and mode into a compact corner or pause menu. Hide technical debug behind an F3-style toggle. Make low health, hunger, effects, and selected slot readable without relying on color alone. This item is complete when there are no HUD collisions from 320 to 1920 CSS pixels, primary icons are not emoji, debug is off by default, survival information is readable over bright and dark terrain, focus and screen-reader labels exist for clickable controls, and the world receives noticeably more visual space.

### 11. Add a title screen, pause menu, settings, and staged onboarding

Initialization currently enters world generation immediately and the controls overlay is the only real meta-game surface (`js/main.js:25-152`). Create a title screen with Continue Candy World, New World, world selection, and Settings. Continue should appear only for a valid save; creating or deleting a world must require confirmation; the game must communicate save status and recovery clearly. Escape should enter a true paused state with Resume, Settings, Controls, Save status, Return to Title, and accessibility options. New players should receive a short, dismissible tutorial sequence that teaches looking and moving, mining, placing, inventory, crafting, and taming without permanently occupying the HUD. Provide sensitivity, FOV, render scale, view distance, volume, HUD scale, key remapping, bob/shake, flashing, and reduced-motion settings. This item is complete when fresh and returning flows differ correctly, world replacement cannot happen accidentally, simulation and held inputs pause reliably, settings and tutorial progress persist, pointer lock always restores correctly, and every menu can be completed with keyboard alone.

### 12. Design a deliberate and rewarding first ten minutes

The current spawn search chooses a high nearby surface but does not create a readable clearing, and the reviewed game opened beneath a large lollipop canopy (`js/main.js:4-22`, `js/world.js:201-213`). The starting inventory also provides abundant stone, planks, glass, a crafting table, and decorative blocks, skipping much of Minecraft's foundational learning loop (`js/player.js:250-259`). Generate a safe clearing with an unobstructed horizon, a nearby tree, surface resources, animals, and a modest candy waystone that provides direction without free high-tier loot. In standard Survival, start with empty hands and guide the player through gathering wood, making planks, crafting a table, creating a tool, preparing a shelter, and obtaining a treat. Cozy Survival may offer a small picnic basket and gentler prompts. This item is complete when ten fresh seeded worlds always spawn safely above land, outside foliage and water, with required early resources reachable; tutorial milestones can be completed without luck; the player understands the core loop within ten minutes; and returning saves never have their spawn area regenerated destructively.

### 13. Separate Survival, Cozy Survival, and Creative world rules

Survival currently permits double-space flight and an immediate `C` Creative toggle, which makes hunger, tools, rewards, and progression optional without clearly declaring that choice (`js/input.js:119-130,165-168`, `js/player.js:513-588`). Add a persistent `worldRules` structure selected when creating a world. Standard Survival should disable flight, prevent casual mode switching, use normal damage and progression, and optionally drop inventory on death. Cozy Survival should preserve the same gathering/crafting loop while allowing keep-inventory, reduced or non-lethal enemy damage, optional starter supplies, and gentler hunger. Creative should retain flight, infinite resources, and instant breaking. Offer an explicitly confirmed administrative mode change from settings rather than a hidden keyboard cheat, and migrate old saves to behavior matching their prior state. This item is complete when each mode has testable, documented rules; standard Survival cannot accidentally fly or enter Creative; Cozy assists are independently configurable; Creative remains effortless; quests do not award Survival progression in Creative unless designed to; and all rules survive save/load.

### 14. Add harvest tiers, correct loot tables, and tool durability

Tools currently modify only break speed, swords are effectively poor mining tools, and successful mining attempts to add the exact block ID directly to inventory (`js/tools.js:4-40`, `js/render.js:93-108`). Expand the block and item registries with stable keys and properties such as hardness, preferred tool, minimum harvest tier, drop table, experience or progression value, durability, attack damage, and special behavior. Stone and ores should yield configured resources rather than universally yielding themselves; wrong-tier tools should produce nothing or low-value crumbs; leaves, glass, and specialty blocks should have deliberate drop rules; and Creative should bypass restrictions. Display remaining durability without clutter and provide a clear break animation/sound. This item is complete when wood→stone→iron→diamond/netherite progression changes what can be harvested, every registered mineable block has an explicit tested loot rule, durability decrements exactly once per successful action, broken tools disappear safely, wrong tools never duplicate valuable resources, and old inventory records migrate with sensible durability defaults.

### 15. Add physical item entities, merging, and pickup behavior

At present a mined block is added directly to inventory before the world block is removed, which means a full inventory can prevent mining entirely (`js/render.js:93-108`). Add lightweight item entities with stable IDs, item/count data, position and velocity, gravity and ground collision, a short pickup delay, bob/spin presentation, nearby-stack merging, magnet attraction, expiry, and a hard global/local cap. Drops should emerge from broken blocks, defeated mobs, discarded inventory, and broken storage containers. Pickups should use transactional inventory insertion and leave any remainder in the world. Determine which nearby drops need persistence, or serialize them by chunk with bounded lifetimes. This item is complete when full inventory never prevents a block from breaking, partial pickup preserves excess counts, nearby matching stacks merge without duplication, item entities cannot fall through one-block floors during hitches, leaving and returning to a recently saved area restores eligible drops, and hundreds of drops degrade gracefully rather than stalling rendering or simulation.

### 16. Generate connected caves and move Deep Dark underground

Chunk generation currently fills nearly every coordinate below the heightmap with bedrock, stone, filler, or ore, so underground exploration is equivalent to tunneling through a solid mass (`js/world.js:126-161`). Add a deterministic generation pipeline of base terrain, 3D density cave carving, biome/strata decoration, connected tunnels and chambers, ravines, ore veins, and underground structures. Protect bedrock, keep spawn columns safe, and make cave decisions chunk-local or border-stable so generation order cannot create seams. Reinterpret Deep Dark as a discovered underground region with frosting-sculk growth, vibration-sensitive danger, rare loot, and striking low-light art. Add rock-candy geodes, caramel drips, chocolate caverns, and abandoned wafer mines. This item is complete when every evaluated seed has at least one reachable cave entrance within a reasonable spawn radius, caves cross chunk borders seamlessly, ore distribution varies meaningfully with depth, the underground Deep Dark is not exposed as an ordinary surface biome, lighting makes exploration materially different, and generation remains repeatable from seed and generator version.

### 17. Implement child-configurable combat, damage, death, and respawn

The item registry includes six tiers of swords, hearts are displayed, and a Warden exists, but mob state has no health, damage, aggression, or loot fields and AI only idles, wanders, follows, or flees (`js/items.js:5-14`, `js/mobs.js:562-585,853-1029`). Add entity targeting that fairly arbitrates primary input between attacking a visible mob and mining a block; implement attack cooldowns, reach, line-of-sight, damage, knockback, invulnerability frames, hurt feedback, death, and loot. Add player fall, drowning, and hostile damage, plus a respawn screen and recoverable inventory drops under applicable rules. Use playful, non-gory enemies such as sour gummies, licorice spiders, or marshmallow archers. This item is complete when combat is frame-rate independent, walls prevent hits, swords differ by tier, mobs visibly react and die exactly once, player death returns to a valid bed or world spawn, inventory behavior follows world rules, Peaceful removes hostile damage without disabling exploration, and Gentle mode prevents unexpectedly harsh outcomes.

### 18. Make day and night a complete gameplay cycle

The existing ten-minute cycle changes sky color and global daylight but has little influence on spawning, shelter, navigation, farming, or objectives (`js/effects.js:8-41`). Add a spawn manager that considers time, local light, sky exposure, biome, player distance, entity caps, and protected areas. Dusk should give an audible and visual warning; dark areas should become risky; suitable enemies should retreat, burn, or despawn at dawn according to their design; and sleeping in a marshmallow bed should set spawn and advance safely to morning when conditions allow. Connect crop growth, villager schedules, ambient audio, and quests to world time. This item is complete when darkness—not arbitrary timers—controls eligible spawning, nothing hostile appears beside the player, a bed, or a waystone, sleeping consistently advances the clock and save state, night remains navigable with lighting, daytime is not empty, and Peaceful/Cozy rules modify danger without breaking the cycle.

### 19. Replace flat recipe lists with shaped player and table crafting

Current recipes are unordered ingredient lists, and the UI explicitly tells players that ingredients can be anywhere (`js/crafting.js:3-74`, `index.html:45-53`). Preserve the helpful recipe browser but add a real 2×2 player grid and 3×3 crafting-table grid with pattern/key recipe definitions, mirrored patterns where intended, stack consumption, output preview, shift-crafting, and safe remainder handling. Keep candy-specific recipes approachable and allow discovered recipes to autofill when ingredients exist, especially in Cozy mode. Model recipes in one data schema rather than separate UI-only arrays. This item is complete when spatial arrangement matters, table-only patterns cannot be crafted from the player grid, mirrored recipes work only when declared, output cannot be duplicated through rapid clicks or full inventories, grid contents survive UI transitions or return safely, keyboard operation is possible, and every legacy recipe has been deliberately migrated or retired.

### 20. Turn the furnace into a persistent timed Candy Oven

Furnace recipes currently complete immediately when clicked and do not consume fuel or store world state (`js/crafting.js:63-70,199-218,240-415`). Add block-entity state keyed by world position with input, fuel, output, burn time, recipe progress, and last simulation time. Create a distinct oven interface with clear slots and progress indicators, support fuel values for wood and future candy fuels, pause when output is blocked, and show a lit block texture plus glow, smoke, and sound while active. Decide how offline elapsed time is bounded so reopening a save cannot create extreme production or exploit clock changes. This item is complete when fuel is consumed predictably, valid recipes take time, invalid or blocked states pause safely, contents and progress survive save/load and future chunk unloading, breaking an oven returns its contents through item drops, active visuals match actual state, and tests cover reloads, insufficient fuel, output overflow, and schema migration.

### 21. Add core utility and architectural blocks

The block registry ends at 32 types and lacks the utility pieces that turn a temporary shelter into a personalized base (`js/blocks.js:3-48`). Add cupcake chests, wafer doors, candy-cane fences, ladders, signs, slabs, stairs, marshmallow beds, and lollipop lamps. Introduce placement orientation and block state without exploding the base block-ID range, plus partial collision and selection shapes for non-cubes. Chests need transactional persistent inventories; doors must toggle and collide correctly; fences should connect to neighbors; signs require sanitized text; beds must integrate with sleep and respawn; and lamps must integrate with block light and optional redstone-lite power. This item is complete when all utilities craft, place, orient, interact, collide, save, reload, and drop correctly; adjacent visual states update across chunk borders; storage cannot duplicate items; text entry does not leak game key presses; and structures built with slabs/stairs remain navigable by both player and mobs.

### 22. Add renewable farming, crop growth, and animal breeding

The current food loop is mostly mining, crafting, trading, and immediate treat consumption, so long-term Survival lacks renewable homestead play (`js/crafting.js:15-18,52-60`, `js/mobs.js:1504-1531`). Add a hoe, frosting soil, hydration, cookie-wheat, sugar cane, gummy berries, and other Candy World crops represented by bounded growth-stage state. Drive growth from scheduled fixed ticks using light, water, and biome rules rather than per-frame random checks. Harvesting should yield both food ingredients and replanting material. Feeding two compatible adult animals should trigger affection feedback and create a baby after a cooldown, with population caps and saved breeding state. This item is complete when a player can establish a renewable treat supply without Creative or trading, crops grow reproducibly within reasonable ranges, unloaded chunks do not require continuous simulation, harvesting and breeding cannot duplicate resources, babies mature safely, pens do not destroy performance, and relevant quests teach the loop without forcing it.

### 23. Expand exploration with deterministic structures and contained loot

Waystones and small villager houses already prove the engine can place deterministic landmarks, but exploration still lacks enough long-distance goals (`js/world.js:276-501`, `js/mobs.js:811-850`). Generalize structure placement into reusable templates with biome/depth predicates, spacing, rotation, bounding-box collision checks, generator versioning, and seeded loot. Add gingerbread villages, wafer mineshafts, cupcake shrines, candy castles, sprinkle geodes, licorice dungeons, and rare giant dessert formations. Put rewards in persistent chests or interactions rather than exposed ore blocks, and ensure loot tiers respect progression. This item is complete when at least four structure families generate under distinct rules, structures do not overlap spawn or each other improperly, generation is identical regardless of chunk order, opened loot remains opened, maps or clues can lead to rare sites, every structure has a gameplay reason to visit, and no template creates unavoidable suffocation or impassable terrain.

### 24. Add a deliberately bounded redstone-lite candy mechanism system

Minecraft's construction depth comes partly from making builds respond, not merely exist. Add a small original mechanism set: jelly-wire, sugar switches, frosting pressure plates, repeaters or delay candies, lollipop lamps, powered doors, and perhaps note-candy blocks. Represent binary power and scheduled updates explicitly, with a strict per-tick work budget and cycle protection. Avoid attempting the full complexity or quirks of Minecraft redstone; the goal is understandable family-friendly contraptions such as automatic doors, light switches, simple traps, and musical sequences. Integrate mechanisms with block state, persistence, neighbor updates, and chunk boundaries. This item is complete when a switch can power connected wire and a lamp or door, repeaters provide deterministic delay/direction, loops cannot freeze the game, unloading/reloading preserves state, breaking components updates networks safely, tutorial examples can be built from Survival recipes, and the system remains original in appearance and terminology.

### 25. Turn the Quest Book into a branching advancement and teaching system

The existing ten quests are mostly independent counters, and some rewards skip progression—for example, taming a unicorn can immediately award diamonds (`js/quests.js:18-99`). Preserve the friendly Quest Book but divide it into Survival, Explorer, Builder, Chef, Mechanic, and Pet branches with dependencies, optional side paths, discovery states, and recipe or cosmetic unlocks. The main Survival path should teach gathering wood, crafting tools, shelter, oven use, sleeping, iron, caves, lights, farming, and structures without becoming a mandatory checklist for experienced players. Use event-driven progress rather than scattering new hooks. This item is complete when a new player can follow a coherent path from empty hands to a stable base and first deep expedition, rewards never bypass the tier they teach, Creative progress is handled intentionally, optional branches stay optional, completed state and claimed rewards migrate safely, the UI explains prerequisites, and every quest has an automated progress test plus a browser-visible completion check.

### 26. Deepen villagers, animals, pets, and ambient world reactions

Mob models and animations are among the project's strongest features, but behavior is still dominated by idle, wander, follow, and flee states (`js/mobs.js:853-1029`). Add short reusable reactions such as blinking, grazing, pecking, greeting, trading nods, tame hops, hurt responses, sleep, and curiosity. Give villagers professions, schedules, gathering places, workstation associations, persistent reputation, and trades that evolve without rerolling unpredictably. Give pets sit/follow/wander commands, affection feedback, robust owner catch-up, safe teleport recovery, and visible response to renaming. Improve navigation beyond column snapping so mobs understand steps, walls, drops, water, fences, and doors. This item is complete when every passive species has at least two distinctive ambient behaviors, villager identity and trades persist deterministically, pets obey commands within 250 milliseconds and cannot be lost to ordinary terrain, state survives save/load, reactions have matching audio/particles, and entity updates remain within the budget at the existing 80-mob cap.

### 27. Make inventory and station management fast, tactile, and accessible

This item is complete when every common inventory operation matches a documented rule, rapid input cannot duplicate or delete items, closing any interface safely returns cursor contents, shift-click chooses predictable destinations, tooltips fit small screens, Creative search is responsive, 200% HUD scale remains usable, and automated transaction tests cover full, partial, and malformed inventories.

### 28. Add deterministic seeds, generator versions, and multiple world slots

Terrain hashes are deterministic but accept no explicit world seed, while villager trades, mob placement, and related generated content use `Math.random()` and are not fully represented in the save (`js/noise.js:3-30`, `js/mobs.js:554-590`, `js/persistence.js:606-630`). Introduce `worldSeed`, `generatorVersion`, and independent deterministic random streams derived from seed, chunk coordinates, and feature type. Make structure, villager, trade, and persistent variant generation independent of chunk generation order.
